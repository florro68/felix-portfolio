How I Capture Wild Horses Without Disturbing Them

When I photograph wild horses, I always keep one principle in mind: respect the subject.

  • I scout quietly from a distance before shooting.
  • I use longer focal lengths (like 120mm+) to avoid approaching too close.
  • I move slowly and deliberately — never running, never surprising.
  • I observe body language. If the herd seems agitated, I stop immediately.
  • I often spend hours without taking a shot, just learning their patterns.

Photographing wild horses is not just about the image. It’s about trust, timing, and letting their rhythm lead mine. This patience builds honesty in my work.

– Felix I. Flores

Why I Photograph in Black and White

Some scenes don't need color — they need truth.

Black and white strips an image to its essence. Light, shadow, form — all come forward with clarity. When I remove color, I remove distraction.

I use this approach for moments that are more about emotion than detail. A quiet fog over a moor. The starkness of a lone stallion in winter. These are stories told better without color.

Shooting in black and white forces me to see differently. I think about contrast, texture, and tone. I slow down and shoot with more intent.

For me, black and white is not nostalgia — it's focus. It's discipline. It's truth.

– Felix I. Flores
